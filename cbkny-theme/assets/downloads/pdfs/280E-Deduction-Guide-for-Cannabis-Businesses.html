What is IRC 280E?

Understanding the Tax Code That Affects Your Business

The Basics of 280E

Internal Revenue Code Section 280E was enacted in 1982 to prevent businesses from deducting ordinary business expenses if they traffic in controlled substances. For cannabis businesses, this means:

  • No ordinary business expense deductions - Marketing, advertising, rent, utilities, etc.
  • Only COGS deductions allowed - Cost of Goods Sold is your only deduction
  • Applies to all cannabis businesses - Regardless of state legality
  • Federal law supersedes state law - Even in legal states
üí° Key Takeaway

280E essentially means cannabis businesses pay taxes on their gross profit (revenue minus COGS) rather than their net profit (revenue minus all expenses). This can result in effective tax rates of 70% or higher.

Why 280E Exists

Congress enacted 280E to prevent drug dealers from claiming business expense deductions. The law was intended to:

  • Discourage illegal drug trafficking
  • Prevent tax benefits for illegal activities
  • Maintain the integrity of the tax system

However, the law was written broadly and now affects legal cannabis businesses in states where cannabis is legalized.

Who 280E Affects

280E applies to any business that "traffics" in cannabis, including:

  • Cultivation facilities - Growing and harvesting cannabis
  • Processing facilities - Manufacturing cannabis products
  • Dispensaries - Retail cannabis sales
  • Distributors - Cannabis product distribution
  • Testing laboratories - Cannabis testing services

Note: Ancillary businesses that don't directly handle cannabis may not be subject to 280E.

COGS: Your Only Deduction

Maximizing Cost of Goods Sold Under 280E

What Qualifies as COGS

Under 280E, you can only deduct Cost of Goods Sold (COGS). COGS includes:

  • Direct materials - Seeds, soil, nutrients, packaging materials
  • Direct labor - Growing, harvesting, processing labor
  • Production facility costs - Allocated portion of rent, utilities, maintenance
  • Quality control and testing - Lab testing, quality assurance
  • Inventory carrying costs - Storage, security, insurance on inventory
üìä COGS Calculation Example

Scenario: Cannabis cultivation facility

  • Direct materials: $50,000
  • Direct labor: $30,000
  • Facility allocation (50%): $25,000
  • Testing costs: $5,000
  • Total COGS: $110,000
COGS Allocation Methods

For mixed-use facilities, you must allocate costs between COGS and non-deductible expenses:

  • Square footage method - Allocate based on space used for production
  • Time allocation - Allocate based on time spent on production activities
  • Revenue allocation - Allocate based on revenue generated from production
  • Direct cost method - Allocate based on direct costs incurred

Important: Choose one method and apply it consistently.

Maximizing COGS Deductions

To maximize your COGS deductions:

  • Track all direct costs - Don't miss any production-related expenses
  • Optimize allocation methods - Use the most favorable allocation method
  • Document everything - Maintain detailed records of all COGS calculations
  • Review regularly - Ensure your COGS calculations are accurate and complete
üí° Pro Tip

Consider restructuring your business to maximize COGS. For example, separate cultivation from retail operations to optimize cost allocations.

Non-Deductible Expenses

What You Can't Deduct Under 280E

Common Non-Deductible Expenses
‚ùå These Expenses Are NOT Deductible
  • Marketing and advertising - All promotional activities
  • General administrative expenses - Office rent, utilities, supplies
  • Management fees - Executive compensation, consulting fees
  • Legal fees - General legal services (not related to production)
  • Insurance - General liability, property insurance
  • Professional services - Accounting, bookkeeping (not related to production)
  • Travel and entertainment - Business travel, meals, entertainment
  • Software and technology - General business software, computers
The Cost of Non-Deductible Expenses
üìä Tax Impact Example

Scenario: Cannabis dispensary with $1M revenue

  • COGS: $400,000
  • Operating expenses: $300,000 (non-deductible)
  • Taxable income: $600,000 (revenue - COGS)
  • Tax at 35%: $210,000
  • Effective tax rate: 21% of revenue

Without 280E: Tax would be $105,000 (35% of $300,000 net income)

280E Impact: $105,000 additional tax burden

Strategies to Minimize Non-Deductible Expenses

While you can't eliminate non-deductible expenses, you can minimize their impact:

  • Multi-entity structure - Separate production from other activities
  • Cost allocation optimization - Maximize COGS allocations
  • Business restructuring - Separate deductible from non-deductible activities
  • Efficient operations - Reduce overall expenses where possible

Multi-Entity Strategy

Structuring Your Business for Tax Efficiency

How Multi-Entity Structures Work

A multi-entity structure separates your cannabis business into multiple entities:

  • Cefense LLC - Holds the cannabis license and handles production
  • Management LLC - Provides administrative and management services
  • Real Estate LLC - Owns and leases property to the cannabis entity
  • IP LLC - Holds intellectual property and licenses it to the cannabis entity
üí° How It Helps

Non-cannabis entities can deduct ordinary business expenses, while the cannabis entity focuses on maximizing COGS deductions.

Common Multi-Entity Structures
üè¢ Structure Example 1: Cultivation + Management
  • Cannabis Cultivation LLC - Grows cannabis (280E applies)
  • Management Services LLC - Provides management services (deductible)
  • Real Estate LLC - Owns facility and leases to cultivation (deductible)
üè™ Structure Example 2: Retail + Services
  • Cannabis Retail LLC - Sells cannabis (280E applies)
  • Marketing Services LLC - Provides marketing services (deductible)
  • Technology LLC - Provides software and tech services (deductible)
Implementation Considerations

Before implementing a multi-entity structure:

  • Consult with professionals - Legal and tax advisors
  • Understand state regulations - Some states restrict multi-entity structures
  • Maintain proper separation - Keep entities truly separate
  • Document everything - Maintain detailed records of all transactions
  • Consider costs - Additional legal and accounting costs
‚ö†Ô∏è Important Considerations

Multi-entity structures are complex and require careful implementation. They must be legitimate business structures, not just tax avoidance schemes. Always consult with qualified professionals.

Documentation Best Practices

What to Keep and How to Organize It

Essential Documentation

Maintain detailed documentation for all tax-related decisions:

  • COGS calculations - Detailed breakdowns of all COGS components
  • Cost allocation methods - Documentation of allocation methodologies
  • Business structure decisions - Legal documents and business purposes
  • Professional advice - Letters from CPAs, attorneys, and consultants
  • Industry research - Articles, studies, and industry reports
Record Retention Requirements

IRS record retention requirements:

  • Tax returns - Keep indefinitely
  • Supporting documents - 7 years from filing date
  • Business records - 7 years from transaction date
  • Legal documents - Keep indefinitely
  • Professional correspondence - 7 years
Organization System

Organize your documentation for easy access:

  • Digital filing system - Cloud-based storage with backup
  • Chronological organization - By tax year and transaction date
  • Category-based filing - Separate folders for different types of documents
  • Index and search - Easy to find specific documents
  • Regular backups - Multiple copies in different locations
Audit Defense Documentation

Prepare for potential audits by maintaining:

  • Business purpose documentation - Why decisions were made
  • Industry benchmarking - How your practices compare to industry standards
  • Professional opinions - Written opinions from qualified professionals
  • Consistency documentation - Evidence of consistent application of policies
  • Change documentation - Why and when policies were changed
